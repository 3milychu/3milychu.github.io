Interactive Mapping

Find That Recent Earthquake
USGS Earthquake Hazards Program, 2017

This web app was created as an exercise in using public data to make meaningful spatial representations of data. I used USGS Earthquake Hazard Program's data feed of earthquakes occuring in the last 30 days using the interactive map library Leaflet. The slider selects earthquakes within 0.04 magnitude of the slider value. Users are able to dive into an interesting earthquake down to the neighborhood of origin.

With Mercator, everything changed. That's like thinking, if there was an earthquake in the olden times - they really thought it happened right there. We know better now. It's like a diagonal slice of pie and the bullseye is just the crust.

This exercise focused on mapping the most dynamic and interesting variable in an earthquake dataset from USGS. The goal was to use colors to differentiate among variety in one variable. I narrowed down variables of social interest to "depth" and "magnitude". Using RStudio, it was easy to see that the variable "magnitude" held the strongest data visualization potential. While earthquakes, especially those most significant, are a serious matter, I wanted to gamify the exploration of it in a lighthearted manner. The design hoped to encourage understanding of where low and high earthquakes most frequently occur in our world through play. The technical challenge was using satellite imagery for the actual web app. Nimble ability to zoom in and out was a priority. This way, participants can discover the terrain and neighborhoods where earthquakes take place. Sometimes yes, in the ocean, mountains or deserts. When earthquakes occured in residential areas, I found it uncanny that you could locate the specificity of its epicenter under a house.

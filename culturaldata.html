Cultural Data

Met.erials
Parsons MSDV Major Studio, 2018

This project analyzed 155k objects containing the Metropolitan Musem of Art's top seven occuring materials. Questions asked: What are representative items made from each material in the MET's collection? What larger patterns are they a part of, and what can we learn from the semiology of cultural artifacts?

I am interested in how things came to be, and what traditions they are a part of - it helps me understand how the look and feel of what I design taps into an overarching narrative that supercedes my moment in time.

Background This project was created in the Major Studio I course within Parson's MS Data Visualization program in partnership with the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City, NY. We worked with the museum's General Manager of Collection Information to understand the nature of and nuances behind the data - including missing gaps and where the most reliable information was recorded consistently in the dataset. Direction I landed on the question of the concept of "Met.erials" after conducting an initial data analysis in R. The obvious was confirmed - for example, there are a lot of musical instruments made out of wood. There were other findings more interesting: we've been using leather as a species since the beginning of civilization (2000 BCE), mostly through clothing. Adding steel to the mix catapults resulting objects' use for Arms and Armor. Silver represents perhaps the most mediums in the MET's collection, most likely due to its inclusion in photographs, which makes up the largest number of objects in the chosen dataset. Steel and Bronze are equalizers: they represent the most number of continents out of all mediums selected. There are other stories to piece together as well, and which I hope to give other designers, makers, and storytellers access to explore at their leisure.

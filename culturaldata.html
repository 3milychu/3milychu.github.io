Cultural Data

What Makes Art?
Parsons MSDV Major Studio, 2018

What artifacts of civilization are ultimately remembered as "art?" How has the creation of objects evolved over time, and how do the patterns illuminate humanity's journey through time and space? Using a selected dataset of 8,000 objects from the MET's collection, this project asks how timeless materials we see around us everyday came to be, and the traditions they are part of.

Launch Prototype
.

I am interested in how things came to be, and what traditions they are a part of - it helps me understand how the look and feel of what I design taps into an overarching narrative that supercedes my moment in time.

Background

This project was created in the Major Studio I course within Parson's MS Data Visualization program in partnership with the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City, NY. We worked with the museum's General Manager of Collection Information to understand the nature of and nuances behind the data - including missing gaps and where the most reliable information was recorded consistently in the dataset.

Direction

I landed on the question of "What Makes Art?" after conducting an initial data analysis in R. The obvious was confirmed: you find lots of zinc in museum objects because of their photographs, and there are a lot of musical instruments made out of wood. There were other findings more interesting: we've been using leather as a species since the beginning of civilization (2000 BCE), mostly through clothing. When we added steel to the mix, it gave us the explosion in arms and armor we see between the 1400s and 1600s. Germans held the lead on number of objects made out of leather, a position the Americans took over when you add steel to the mix. There are other stories to piece together as well, and which I hope to give other designers, makers, and storytellers access to explore at their leisure.
